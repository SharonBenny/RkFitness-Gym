news about fitness

  • Benefits of Exercise

    We have all heard it many times before - regular exercise is good for you, and it can help you lose weight. But if you are like many Americans, you are busy, you have a sedentary job, and you haven't yet changed your exercise habits. The good news is that it's never too late to start. You can start slowly, and find ways to fit more physical activity into your life. To get the most benefit, you should try to get the recommended amount of exercise for your age. If you can do it, the payoff is that you will feel better, help prevent or control many diseases, and likely even live longer.

  • Do you walk very slowly? Here's some bad news for you

    Walking is the easiest and the best form of exercise that one can do to improve their overall health, increase longevity and functional years. Another good thing about walking is that it has the lowest injury rate as compared to any other exercise, claims the 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines Advisory Committee. But the benefit of any physical activity depends on three elements – duration, intensity and frequency of exercise. A recent study published in the Journal of American Geriatrics shows that walking too slowly can foreshadow future problems that could prevent a person from being fully mobile. It was found that slow walking speed under both usual-pace and complex conditions was associated with a greater risk of developing mobility issues. Most of the participants in the study who reported having mobility issues were females having diabetes, knee pain, depression and were obese. Further, the researches analysed information from the Health Aging and Body Composition study of adults from the US between 1997 to 1998. All the 337 participants were between the age of 70 to 79 and had no difficulty in walking a quarter mile or climbing 10 steps without resting.

Jim Stoppani's Encyclopedia of Muscle and Strength

Jim Stoppani, PhD, received his doctorate in exercise physiology with a minor in biochemistry from the University of Connecticut. After graduation, he served as a postdoctoral research fellow in the prestigious John B. Pierce Laboratory and department of cellular and molecular physiology at Yale University School of Medicine, where he investigated the effects of exercise and diet on gene regulation in muscle tissue. For his groundbreaking research he was awarded the Gatorade Beginning Investigator in Exercise Science Award in 2002 by the American Physiological Society. From 2002 to 2013 Stoppani was senior science editor for Muscle and Fitness, Muscle and Fitness Hers, and Flex magazines. He is currently the owner of the JYM Supplement Science and jimstoppani.com. Dr. Stoppani is the creator of the popular training and nutrition programs Shortcut to Size and Shortcut to Shred, Dr. Stoppani has been the personal nutrition and health consultant for numerous celebrity clients, such as LL Cool J, Dr. Dre, Mario Lopez, and Chris Pine.
